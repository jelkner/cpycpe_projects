Decimal Numbers

We humans use a decimal numbers system in which number values from 0 to 9 are represented by unique characters and positional notation reuses these characters to represent all whole numbers larger than 9.

The following table shows the place values of decimal numbers.

Decminal Number Place Values
104 103 102 101 100
10000 1000 100 10 1

Using this system, the number written as 4275 means 4 x 1000 + 2 x 100 + 7 x 10 + 5 x 1.

Binary Numbers

Computers, for reasons that stem primarily from the concerns of electrical engineering, store numbers using a binary, or base-2 number system. In the binary number system, there are only two digits, 0 and 1, and numbers are represented using a positional notation of powers of two.

The table below shows the first 10 place values of binary numbers.

Binary Number Place Values
29 28 27 26 25 24 23 22 21 20
512 256 128 64 32 16 8 4 2 1

There was a ThinkGeek t-shirt that read, There are 10 types of people in the world: Those who understand binary and those who don't. The joke is that 102 in binary is the number 2.

Let's look at a few examples to give you an idea of how the binary number system works.

  • 4 in decimal is 100 in binary.
  • 5 in decimal is 101 (4 + 1) in binary.
  • 34 in decimal is 100010 (32 + 2) in binary.
  • 15 in decimal is 1111 (8 + 4 + 2 + 1) in binary.

There are lots of resources available online to help you better understand binary numbers, and if you want to get what this project is doing you are encouraged to pause now and look at some of them.
